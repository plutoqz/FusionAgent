# Chapter 5 Draft: Experiment Results And Analysis

## Chapter Scope

This chapter reports the currently frozen experimental evidence for FusionAgent. The results are organized around the thesis questions introduced in Chapter 1 and the protocol described in Chapter 4:

- `RQ1`: whether KG-grounded planning and validation improve executable planning for bounded vector-fusion tasks.
- `RQ2`: whether bounded governance mechanisms, including validation, fallback, healing, and replan, improve runtime robustness without widening the claim surface.
- `RQ3`: whether the evidence contract makes runs inspectable and reusable for research reporting.

The chapter uses four evidence classes. First, frozen paper-evidence rows support the main runtime and evidence-contract claims. Second, Freeze C controlled ablation evidence compares A0/A1/A2a/A2b/A2c under a deterministic counterfactual protocol. Third, a controlled semi-real quality supplement checks whether deterministic fusion adapters produce structurally valid outputs on four bounded task-family fixtures. Fourth, a Caracas real-data supplement checks structural quality on a separate multi-domain real dataset.

The main boundary is important: the controlled ablation does not run fresh live API, LLM, or KG calls. A2c rows are copied from inspection-derived full-runtime evidence, while A0, A1, A2a, and A2b are deterministic counterfactual rows generated by `scripts/materialize_controlled_ablation_rows.py`. The controlled quality supplement is also not a real Benin benchmark replacement; it is bounded robustness evidence over deterministic semi-real fixtures. The Caracas supplement improves real-data coverage, but it is a separate benchmark, not the original Benin Freeze B run.

## RQ1: Executable Planning And Runtime Success

The controlled ablation compares five runtime variants:

| Variant | Interpretation |
| --- | --- |
| A0 | Coarse task-to-handler baseline without KG decomposition or validator governance. |
| A1 | KG retrieval context without fail-closed validation. |
| A2a | KG context plus validator report-only behavior. |
| A2b | KG context plus fail-closed validation and KG fallback. |
| A2c | A2b plus policy and healing governance, represented by inspection-derived full-runtime rows. |

The aggregated controlled ablation results are:

| Variant | Cases | Planning valid rate | Unknown algorithm rate | Execution success rate | Avg grounding score |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| A0 | 4 | 0.25 | 0.75 | 0.25 | 0.0 |
| A1 | 4 | 0.75 | 0.0 | 0.75 | 0.375 |
| A2a | 4 | 0.75 | 0.0 | 1.0 | 0.5 |
| A2b | 4 | 0.75 | 0.0 | 1.0 | 0.5 |
| A2c | 4 | 0.75 | 0.0 | 1.0 | 0.5 |

The first pattern is the contrast between A0 and the KG-aware variants. A0 has a planning-valid rate of `0.25` and an unknown-algorithm rate of `0.75`, while A1 through A2c reduce the unknown-algorithm rate to `0.0`. Within the controlled protocol, this supports the bounded interpretation that KG-aware task grounding reduces unsupported algorithm selection relative to a coarse task-to-handler baseline.

The second pattern is the improvement from A1 to A2a/A2b/A2c in execution success. A1 reaches `0.75` execution success, while A2a, A2b, and A2c each reach `1.0`. This indicates that KG context alone is not the entire runtime story. The report-only validator and later governance variants preserve visibility into invalid plans while still allowing the bounded experiment to reach executable outcomes.

The third pattern is grounding. A0 has average grounding score `0.0`; A1 improves to `0.375`; A2a through A2c reach `0.5`. These values should not be overinterpreted as a universal semantic-quality measure. They are useful as a local indicator that the controlled rows carry more explicit KG-grounded planning evidence than the coarse baseline.

Taken together, the controlled ablation supports `RQ1` at a bounded level: KG-aware planning and runtime validation are associated with fewer unknown algorithms and higher executable success over the controlled case set. The result should be reported as evidence for bounded executable planning, not as a claim that the agent can solve arbitrary GIS workflows.

The trace-backed A2c provenance supplement strengthens the interpretation of the A2c rows without changing their scope. Across the four inspection payloads used as A2c sources, `4/4` contain the required audit-event chain (`run_created`, `plan_created`, `plan_validated`, `task_inputs_resolved`, `execution_completed`, `output_schema_validated`, and `run_succeeded`), and `4/4` contain at least one KG trace chain. All four inspections declare output artifacts, but `0/4` artifact files are present in the current workspace checkout. Therefore, the A2c evidence is trace-backed at the inspection level, while artifact-level re-evaluation would require restoring the archived run output files.

## RQ2: Validation, Fallback, And Runtime Governance

The ablation also separates pre-fallback validity from final execution behavior. This distinction matters because a runtime can appear successful while masking invalid plans, or it can reject invalid plans without recovering to a usable result.

| Variant | Validator rejection rate | KG fallback rate | Pre-fallback valid rate | Avg fallback delta |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| A0 | 0.0 | 0.0 | 0.25 | 0.0 |
| A1 | 0.0 | 0.0 | 0.75 | 0.0 |
| A2a | 0.0 | 0.0 | 0.75 | 0.0 |
| A2b | 0.25 | 0.25 | 0.75 | 0.0625 |
| A2c | 0.25 | 0.0 | 0.75 | 0.0 |

A2b is the clearest governance contrast. It has a validator rejection rate of `0.25` and a KG fallback rate of `0.25`, while preserving execution success at `1.0`. In the controlled protocol, this means one invalid pre-fallback case is identified and routed through fallback. This supports the runtime-governance argument: fail-closed validation does not merely reject; it can expose invalidity and connect the run to a bounded recovery route.

A2c also has validator rejection rate `0.25`, but KG fallback rate `0.0` in the inspection-derived rows. This difference should be read carefully. It does not mean A2c lacks fallback capability; it means fallback was not observed in the frozen A2c inspection slice used here. The controlled comparison therefore gives stronger direct evidence for A2b fallback behavior than for observed A2c fallback frequency.

For `RQ2`, the current ablation result supports a bounded governance statement: validation and fallback make invalid pre-fallback behavior visible, and the A2b controlled variant demonstrates recovery-compatible execution for the invalid case in this small controlled set. The stronger thesis claim about healing and replan should continue to cite the separately frozen `c3_replan_fault_recovery` evidence and focused regression tests, rather than relying only on this ablation table.

The C3 recovery governance table makes that separate evidence explicit:

| Condition | Evidence role | Repair allowed | Replan allowed | Observed status | Recovery interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `B0_no_repair_or_replan_boundary` | contrast boundary | False | False | not promoted as independent benchmark | Primary failure remains failed or requires manual intervention. |
| `B1_bounded_healing_replan_verified` | verified recovery row | True | True | passed | Failed execution re-enters through healing, applies revision 2, refreshes inputs when source changes, and succeeds. |
| `G1_replan_grounding_fail_closed` | safety guard | True | True | passed regression | Ungrounded replanned workflow is rejected before execution. |
| `G2_replan_limit_fail_closed` | safety guard | True | bounded by max plan revisions | passed regression | Run fails with `replan_rejected` after the maximum plan revision limit is reached. |

This table improves the C3 claim because it separates success evidence from guard evidence. The verified row supports bounded recovery behavior; the guard rows show why the system should not be described as unlimited self-healing. The no-repair/no-replan row remains a contrast boundary rather than a promoted statistical baseline.

## Evidence Contract And Inspectability

The strongest evidence-contract result is not a single quality number. It is the ability to trace a result back through generated manifests, input rows, summaries, artifacts, and hash-verified output files.

For ablation, `exp-ablation-a0-a2-controlled-comparison` freezes eight files: the original partial A2c inputs and summaries, the controlled input rows, and the controlled summary outputs. The additional `exp-ablation-a0-a2-trace-backed-a2c` manifest freezes a compact provenance table over the four A2c inspection payloads, including inspection hashes, audit-event completeness, KG trace counts, selected decisions, and artifact declarations. This preserves both the derived comparison data and a separate inspection-trace check.

For quality, `exp-quality-freeze-b-controlled-supplement` freezes artifact lineage, four GPKG artifacts, benchmark results, and a human-readable summary. `exp-quality-freeze-b-caracas-real-supplement` freezes five real-data GPKG artifacts, benchmark outputs, readiness, protocol check, and materialization summary. The separate `exp-quality-freeze-b-local-blocker-report` manifest freezes the negative readiness evidence for the original Freeze B benchmark; this is a blocker record, not a benchmark result.

This structure supports `RQ3` in two ways. First, the evidence can be inspected without re-running the full system: the thesis can cite frozen manifests and the corresponding generated tables. Second, the claim boundaries are machine-readable or file-local: the ablation input declares `controlled-ablation-counterfactual-v1`, and the quality benchmark explicitly separates `quality_claim` from `robustness_claim`.

The result is not merely that FusionAgent produces outputs. It produces outputs with enough surrounding evidence to distinguish observed runtime behavior, controlled counterfactual rows, robustness checks, and blocked real-world claims.

## Integrated Interpretation

The controlled ablation and quality supplement point in the same direction, but they answer different questions. The ablation addresses planning and runtime governance: it shows that KG-aware variants avoid unknown algorithms in the controlled set and that validator/fallback governance changes how invalid plans are surfaced. The quality supplement addresses output sanity: it shows that deterministic adapters can produce structurally valid outputs on controlled fixtures.

The distinction matters for the thesis contribution. FusionAgent's agentic contribution is not that it invents new GIS conflation algorithms. The stronger claim is that it constrains the planning and execution lifecycle around those algorithms: task grounding, validation, bounded recovery, and evidence freezing. The current results support that framing better than they would support an overbroad "autonomous GIS agent" claim.

## Current Limitations

Several limitations remain:

- The controlled ablation uses deterministic counterfactual rows for A0/A1/A2a/A2b, not fresh live LLM/API/KG executions.
- The A2c rows come from four smoke inspection payloads, so the sample is small. Their audit and KG traces are frozen, but the declared artifact files are not present in the current workspace checkout.
- The controlled quality supplement uses semi-real deterministic fixtures and cannot replace the original Freeze B real-world cases.
- The Caracas real-data supplement improves real-data evidence, but the building case uses a weak overlap-priority adapter and the water-polygon case is single-source structural sanity.
- The original Freeze B manifest still lacks precomputed artifact paths for five non-smoke cases, and the expected local Benin source root is unavailable in the current environment.
- Fusion quality metrics remain structural and task-specific; they do not establish semantic completeness or operational superiority in a live disaster-response deployment.

These limitations do not invalidate the current results. They define the correct scope: FusionAgent currently has frozen evidence for bounded executable planning, controlled governance comparisons, auditability, controlled robustness checks, and a bounded Caracas real-data structural-quality supplement. Broader real-world superiority claims require the remaining Freeze B artifacts, stronger external baselines, and a larger promoted case set.

## Chapter Summary

The current evidence supports three chapter-level conclusions:

1. KG-aware planning and runtime validation improve controlled executable-planning behavior relative to the coarse A0 baseline, especially by reducing unknown-algorithm selection.
2. Validator and fallback governance make invalid pre-fallback behavior visible and, in the A2b controlled variant, preserve final execution success for the invalid case.
3. The controlled quality supplement shows structurally valid outputs across four bounded semi-real task-family fixtures, and the Caracas supplement adds five real-data structural-quality cases.

The chapter therefore supports the thesis's bounded framing: FusionAgent is best understood as an auditable, KG-grounded runtime for constrained geospatial vector-fusion workflows, not as an unrestricted autonomous GIS system.
